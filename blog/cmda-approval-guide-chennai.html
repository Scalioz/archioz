Understanding CMDA Approval: What Every Chennai Homeowner Needs to Know

Archioz Design Studio · Updated June 2026 · 8 min read

Of everything that can delay a home construction project in Chennai, plan approval is the one homeowners least expect to take this long — and the one that causes the most frustration when it does. Here is a clear, no-jargon explanation of what it actually involves.

What CMDA Approval Actually Is

The Chennai Metropolitan Development Authority (CMDA) is the regulatory body governing construction across the Chennai Metropolitan Area. Before any new construction, addition, or significant alteration can legally begin, your building plan must be approved by the appropriate authority under the Tamil Nadu Town and Country Planning Act. This approval confirms your plan complies with Floor Space Index (FSI) regulations, fire and structural safety standards, setback and height requirements, land use zoning, and road width compliance.

Building without this approval is illegal, full stop — not a minor technicality. Unapproved construction can be demolished, faces penalties, and makes it nearly impossible to get a home loan or resell the property later.

CMDA or GCC? Knowing Which Authority Applies to You

This is one of the most common points of confusion, and getting it wrong wastes real time. The rule is based on building size and location:

  • Ordinary buildings (up to G+3) within Chennai city generally go through the Greater Chennai Corporation (GCC) or the relevant local body.
  • Special buildings (G+4 and above, or over 15 metres in height) go directly to CMDA.
  • Plots outside the Chennai Metropolitan Area boundary — parts of Kanchipuram, Chengalpet outskirts, and similar areas — fall under DTCP via the local Planning Authority instead.

If you're unsure which applies to your plot, this is worth confirming early, since filing with the wrong office can cost you weeks.

Documents You'll Need

A complete application typically requires the following, and missing or outdated documents are one of the most common reasons for delay:

  • Proof of land ownership (sale deed or equivalent)
  • A current Encumbrance Certificate (EC)
  • Patta (land record), which must be up to date at the time of filing
  • Site plan, key plan, floor plans, elevations, and sections
  • Architect or licensed surveyor's certification and registration details

Non-negotiable requirement: all technical drawings must be signed and certified by a Council of Architecture (CoA)-registered architect or a licensed surveyor. Unsigned plans are rejected outright, with no exceptions.

How Long Does It Actually Take?

Under CMDA's revised single-window system, standard residential approvals (G+2, under 300 sq. m) are officially meant to be processed within 30–45 working days. In practice, even with a construction partner managing the process closely, realistic timelines run closer to 6–12 weeks depending on how complete your application is and how quickly any queries are answered. Complex commercial or multi-storey applications can take 3–6 months.

The Process, Step by Step

  • Submit your application through the CMDA or GCC online portal, with all required documents and drawings attached
  • Officials may schedule a site inspection to verify the plan matches ground reality
  • Authorities cross-check your submitted plans against zoning and building code requirements
  • If everything is in order, a demand notice is issued for the applicable fees, calculated based on land use and area
  • Once fees are paid, the approval is granted and a building permit is issued

The Most Common Reasons Applications Get Rejected

Most rejections trace back to one of a small handful of avoidable mistakes:

  • Unsigned or incomplete drawings — missing the architect's certification is an automatic rejection
  • Missing or outdated Encumbrance Certificate or Patta — land records must reflect current status at the time of filing
  • Setback or FSI violations — designs that don't account for required open space margins or exceed permissible built-up area for the plot
  • Mismatched site conditions — when the submitted plan doesn't match what inspectors find on-site

Don't Forget the Completion Certificate

Getting your building plan approved isn't the final step. Once construction is finished exactly per the approved plan, you'll need to apply for a Completion Certificate (CC) — this is what makes the building legally fit for occupancy, and it's frequently overlooked until it becomes a problem at resale or loan time.
